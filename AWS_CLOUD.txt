Cloud Fundamentals:

**********What is Cloud Computing?*****************
Cloud computing means renting IT resources over the internet instead of owning them.

Examples of resources: servers, storage, databases, networking, software.

You pay only for what you use (like electricity or water).

***************2. Benefits of Cloud*******************
Scalability: Grow or shrink resources instantly.

Elasticity: Automatically adjust resources based on demand.

Cost efficiency: No upfront hardware costs, pay‑as‑you‑go.

Global reach: Access services worldwide.

Reliability: Built‑in redundancy and backups.

*****************3. Types of Cloud*******************
Public Cloud: Services offered over the internet (AWS, Azure, GCP).

AWS - amazon web services
Azure - microsoft
GCP   - Google cloud platform

Private Cloud: Cloud infrastructure dedicated to one organization.

Hybrid Cloud: Mix of public + private cloud.

***********************4.Cloud Service Models*******************
IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service): Rent servers, storage, networking (e.g., AWS EC2).

PaaS (Platform as a Service): Rent a platform to build apps without managing servers (e.g., AWS Elastic Beanstalk).

SaaS (Software as a Service): Use ready‑made applications over the internet (e.g., Gmail, Salesforce).


********************AWS Global infrastructure**********************
Region: A big geographical area (like “Mumbai” or “Virginia”) where AWS has servers. You choose a region to run your applications close to your users.

Availability Zone (AZ): Smaller sections inside a region, each with its own independent setup. Using multiple AZs makes your app safe if one goes down.

Data Center: The actual building full of servers and networking equipment. Each AZ is made up of one or more data centers.


*************************Core AWS services***************

Compute (Think: Brains of your app)
EC2 (Elastic Compute Cloud): Virtual servers you can rent. Like renting a computer in the cloud.

Lambda: Run code without servers. You just upload your function, and AWS runs it when needed.

Elastic Beanstalk: A platform that automatically handles servers, scaling, and deployment for your app.

*******************Amazon machine image*********************

An AMI (Amazon Machine Image) is like a snapshot or template of a server in AWS. It contains the operating system, software, configurations, and data you want.

The purpose of creating an AMI is:

✅ To quickly launch new EC2 instances with the exact same setup (instead of reinstalling everything each time).

✅ To back up your current instance so you can restore it later if needed.

✅ To share a preconfigured environment with your team or across regions.

In simple terms: you create an AMI when you want a reusable “blueprint” of your EC2 instance that can be cloned or restored anytime.

An Auto Scaling Group (ASG) in AWS is a service that automatically manages a fleet of EC2 instances.

👉 Purpose: decides how many servers(instances you need) 

It ensures your application always has the right number of servers running.

If traffic increases, it automatically adds more EC2 instances.

If traffic decreases, it shuts down extra instances to save cost.

It also replaces unhealthy instances with new ones to keep your system reliable.

Amazon CloudFront is AWS’s Content Delivery Network (CDN). Its purpose is to deliver your website, videos, APIs, or other content to users quickly and securely by caching copies at servers located around the world (called edge locations).

👉 In simple terms:

It makes your application faster because users get data from the nearest server.

It makes your application more reliable by reducing load on your origin server.

It adds security features like DDoS protection and HTTPS support.

purpose:
RDS is AWS’s managed database service — it runs popular databases like MySQL, PostgreSQL, Oracle, and SQL Server for you.

Instead of installing and maintaining a database yourself, AWS handles backups, updates, and scaling automatically.

You just choose the database type, size, and region, and AWS takes care of the heavy lifting.

Think of it like renting a fully‑maintained library system where you only focus on the books (your data), not the building or staff.

Lambda lets you run code without managing servers. You just upload your function (like a small piece of code), and AWS runs it when triggered.

You only pay for the time your code runs — not for idle servers.


******************EC2 pricing models*******************

4 Ec2 (elastic cloud compute ) models:

1 . On-Demand instances
2 . Reserved instances
3 . saving plans
4 . spot instances

1. On‑Demand Instances
Pay per hour/second with no commitment.

Best for short‑term, unpredictable workloads.

Analogy: Like booking a taxi — you pay only for the ride when you need it.

2. Reserved Instances
Commit for 1 or 3 years to get big discounts (up to ~72%).

Best for steady, predictable workloads.

Analogy: Like buying a bus pass — cheaper if you know y


3. Savings Plans
Flexible pricing model where you commit to a certain amount of usage ($/hour) for 1–3 years.

Works across different instance types and regions.

Analogy: Like a gym membership — you pay monthly, but can use different equipment anytime.

Spot Instances are spare EC2 servers that AWS isn’t currently using.

You can “bid” for them at a huge discount (up to 90% cheaper than On‑Demand).

BUT — if AWS suddenly needs those servers back, your Spot Instance can be stopped with short notice.

Analogy: Imagine booking a hotel room at a flash sale price. You get it cheap, but if the hotel needs the room for a full‑price guest, you may be asked to leave early.



*******************Security and compliance***********

🔑***** IAM (Identity and Access Management)************************************************
What it is: A system to control who can do what in AWS.

Analogy: Think of IAM like the security guard at a company. He checks your ID (user), decides which rooms you can enter (roles/policies), and groups employees by department (groups).

**************Encryption Basics (KMS, CloudHSM)*****************************************************
What it is: Tools to lock your data so only authorized people can open it.

KMS (Key Management Service): AWS’s master key locker — it creates and manages encryption keys for you.

CloudHSM: A dedicated hardware device for managing keys with extra security.

Analogy: Imagine storing valuables in a locker. KMS is like a digital key cabinet managed by AWS, while CloudHSM is like having your own private vault with keys you control.

****************compliance programs*********************************************************************************
Compliance Programs (GDPR, HIPAA, ISO)
What it is: Rules and standards AWS follows to keep data safe and legal.

Examples:

GDPR → European privacy law.

HIPAA → US healthcare data protection.

ISO → International security standards.

Analogy: Like a restaurant following hygiene rules. AWS follows global “data hygiene” rules so customers trust it with sensitive information.


more about IAM:
1. Users
A user is an individual identity (like you or me) with login credentials.

Analogy: Each employee gets their own ID card to enter the office.

2. Groups
A group is a collection of users with the same permissions.

Analogy: The “HR department” group — everyone in HR gets access to HR files automatically.

3. Roles
A role is a temporary identity with specific permissions that AWS services or users can assume.

Analogy: Imagine a visitor badge at the office. You don’t get full employee access, but you can enter certain rooms for a limited time.

4. Policies
A policy is a document that defines what actions are allowed or denied.

Analogy: The office rulebook — “HR staff can open HR files, but not Finance files.”


*************************AWS Price calculator***************************************************************************

Purpose: Helps you estimate how much your AWS setup will cost before you launch it.

Analogy: Like checking the bill estimate at a restaurant before ordering food.

You select services (EC2, S3, RDS, etc.), choose usage, and it shows monthly cost.

Useful for planning budgets and avoiding surprises.

********************AWS cost exploreer***************************************************************************************

AWS Cost Explorer
Purpose: Lets you see and analyze your actual AWS spending over time.

Analogy: Like your bank statement showing where your money went.

You can filter by service, region, or time period to spot trends.

Helps identify which services are costing the most.

********************AWS budgets**********************************************************************************************
WS Budgets
Purpose: Lets you set spending limits and get alerts if you’re close to exceeding them.

Analogy: Like setting a monthly allowance and getting a warning when you’re about to overspend.

Example: “Alert me if my monthly bill goes above $10.”

*******************AWS free tier**********************************************************************************************
AWS Free Tier
Purpose: Allows beginners to use AWS services for free within limits.

Analogy: Like free samples at a store — you can try before you buy.

Examples:

EC2: 750 hours/month of a small instance for 12 months.

S3: 5 GB storage free.

DynamoDB: 25 GB free.

Some services are “Always Free” (like Lambda with 1M requests/month).

------------------------overall summary of pricing and budgets--------------------------------------------
Calculator = estimate before using.

Cost Explorer = track actual spending.

Budgets = set limits and alerts.

Free Tier = safe playground for beginners.

******************************Cloud architecture concepts*************************************************************

1. Scalability
Ability to increase or decrease resources to handle more or fewer users.

Analogy: Like adding more chairs in a restaurant when more customers arrive.

2. Elasticity
System automatically adjusts resources up or down based on demand.

Analogy: A rubber band stretching and shrinking as needed.

Example: An online store adds servers during a sale, then reduces them afterward.

3. High Availability
Designing systems to stay online even if parts fail.

Achieved by using multiple Availability Zones.

Analogy: Having multiple backup generators so the lights never go out.

4. Disaster Recovery
Planning how to restore services quickly after a major failure (like natural disasters).

Methods: Backups, replication, failover systems.

Analogy: Keeping spare keys at a friend’s house in case you lose yours.



*******************************************AWS support plans**************************************************************

1. Basic support(free)
2. Developer support
3. Business support
4. ENterprise support


1. Basic Support (Free)
Included with every AWS account.

Provides access to documentation, whitepapers, and forums.

Analogy: Like buying a gadget and only getting the user manual 


2. Developer Support
Low‑cost plan for experimenting and testing.

Includes email support during business hours and guidance on best practices.

Analogy: Like having a part‑time tutor who answers your questions when they’re available.

3. Business Support
24/7 access to AWS experts via phone, chat, and email.

Includes faster response times and access to Trusted Advisor checks.

Analogy: Like having a full‑time IT support team on call whenever you need them.


4. Enterprise Support
Highest level of support, designed for large organizations.

Includes a dedicated Technical Account Manager (TAM) and proactive guidance.

Analogy: Like hiring a personal consultant who not only fixes problems but also helps plan your future strategy.



✅ Beginner Takeaway
Basic = free, self‑help only.

Developer = affordable help for testing.

Business = 24/7 expert support for production apps.

Enterprise = premium, personalized guidance for big companies.
